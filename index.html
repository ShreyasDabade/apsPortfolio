Domain Introduction

Smart city planning involves the integration of technology and data-driven strategies to improve the quality of life in urban areas. This includes the development of intelligent infrastructure, sustainable practices, and efficient services. The primary goal is to create a more livable, workable, and sustainable urban environment through the use of smart technologies and innovative approaches [1].

Smart cities utilize Internet of Things (IoT) sensors, big data analytics, and artificial intelligence (AI) to monitor and manage urban infrastructure and services efficiently. This integration allows for real-time data collection and analysis, leading to better decision-making and resource allocation. These technologies rely heavily on algorithmic problem-solving techniques to process and analyze large datasets, optimize resources, and predict future trends [2].

Additionally, smart city planning encompasses various domains such as energy management, water supply, waste management, and transportation. These domains work together to enhance the overall functionality and sustainability of urban areas. For instance, smart grids can optimize energy consumption, while smart transportation systems can reduce traffic congestion and improve public transit efficiency. Algorithmic problem-solving is critical in these applications to develop efficient algorithms for managing resources and scheduling [3].

Market Analysis

The global smart city market is experiencing significant growth, driven by the increasing adoption of smart technologies in urban development. According to recent reports, the market is expected to reach USD 2.57 trillion by 2025, with a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 18.4% from 2020 to 2025 [4]. Key areas of investment include smart infrastructure, smart governance, and smart mobility, among others [5].

The adoption of 5G technology is expected to play a crucial role in the development of smart cities by enabling faster and more reliable connectivity. This will facilitate the deployment of IoT devices and support real-time data processing, which are essential for the efficient functioning of smart city systems. Algorithmic problem-solving is integral to these processes, enabling the development of algorithms that can handle real-time data processing and decision-making [6].

Moreover, public-private partnerships are becoming increasingly common in the smart city market. These collaborations help to leverage the strengths of both sectors, leading to more effective and sustainable urban development projects. Investments in smart city initiatives are also driven by the need to address urban challenges such as population growth, resource scarcity, and climate change [7]. Efficient algorithmic solutions are needed to address these challenges through optimization and predictive analytics [8].
